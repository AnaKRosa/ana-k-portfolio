title: How to Brief a Designer (Even If You've Never Done It)

  to know what to say — and what to skip.

Most people overthink the brief. They either write three paragraphs of vague inspiration, or they send a Pinterest board and hope for the best. Neither works. A good brief isn't about knowing design. It's about knowing your business. Here's exactly what to include.

## Start with the problem, not the solution.

The biggest mistake clients make is leading with what they want instead of why they need it. "I want a logo" tells a designer nothing. "I'm launching a wellness brand targeting women 35+ and I need to look premium without feeling clinical" tells them everything.

Before you write anything, answer this: what problem are you trying to solve?

## The five things every brief needs

**1\. Who you are.** One paragraph about your business, what you sell, and who buys it.

**2\. Who your audience is.** Be specific. Not "women" — "women in their 30s who shop at Whole Foods and follow interior design accounts."

**3\. What you need.** Logo, website, social templates — be clear about the deliverable.

**4\. What you like (and don't).** Share 3–5 references and explain why you like them. "I like this because it feels calm" is more useful than just a screenshot.

**5\. What success looks like.** How will you know the project worked? More inquiries? A specific feeling when people land on your site?

## What to leave out.

Don't tell your designer how to design. Trust is part of the process. If you've hired someone whose work you like, let them solve the problem their way. Your job is to define the destination — theirs is to figure out the route.

## The one question that changes everything.

Before you send the brief, ask yourself: if a designer read only this document, would they understand my business well enough to make something right? If the answer is no, keep writing. If yes, you're done.
